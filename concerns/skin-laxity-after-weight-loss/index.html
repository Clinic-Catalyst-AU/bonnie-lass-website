Why the face empties first

The face carries discrete fat pads that give it structure - the cheeks, the temples, the area under the eyes. When you lose weight quickly, those go early. The result is a face that reads as gaunt or hollow rather than lean, and a jawline that has softened rather than sharpened.

And the skin does not shrink to meet it

Skin has a finite ability to retract, and that ability falls with age and with the speed of the loss. Lose a significant amount quickly - and the current generation of weight-loss medications makes that far more common - and the skin is left with more surface area than it has structure to fill. That is laxity, and it is not a moral failing or something you can exercise away.

Why more filler is usually the wrong answer

The instinct is to fill the hollows. Done badly, that gives you a face that is full but still slack, which is the look everyone is afraid of. The face needs structure restored in the right places and the skin itself rebuilt - and those are two different jobs, done in an order.

What we would actually do

Tracey assesses the whole face rather than the hollow you have been staring at. Typically that means restoring structure where it was genuinely lost, and separately rebuilding collagen and skin quality so the skin has something to hold on to. The goal is that you read as well, not as filled.

The questions people actually ask

Straight answers

I am still losing weight. Should I wait?

Usually yes, at least until you are close to stable. Treating a face that is still changing means chasing a moving target. Come in for the free assessment anyway - Tracey will tell you honestly when to start, and that conversation costs nothing.

Is this the 'Ozempic face' thing?

That is the tabloid name for it, and yes. It is not specific to any one medication - it happens with any rapid, significant weight loss. What matters is that it is a known, treatable pattern rather than something wrong with you.

Will people be able to tell I have had something done?

They should not. The aim of the treatment is that people say you look well or rested, not that they can identify what you had.
